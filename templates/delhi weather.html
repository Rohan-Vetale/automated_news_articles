Delhi: North India shivers under the grip of cold wave

India's capital city, Delhi, has grappled for years with a persistent air pollution problem, which has often been likened to living in a gas chamber. Unrelenting smog, clustered with harmful pollutants, paints a grayish-white hue across this city of 20 million people, threatening their health, life expectancy, and quality of life. Under the backdrop of this crisis, a comprehensive review by the University of Surrey, in collaboration with regional government officials in Delhi, asserts that a solution may lie in harnessing the assistance of Delhi's rural neighbors. The findings have been published in the prestigious journal Sustainable Horizons. The air pollution crisis in Delhi is a combined product of multiple factors, including vehicular fumes, industrial pollution, agricultural burning, and particulate matter from construction works. What's noteworthy are the contributions made to this crisis by external factors, specifically the burning of agricultural residue in the neighboring rural areas, termed as 'crop burning.' Studies have linked this seasonal phenomenon, extending from late October through November, to a significant rise in the city's pollution levels. Crop burning releases particles and gases such as carbon dioxide, nitrogen oxides, and particulate matter into the atmosphere, which not only intensify the potent smog engulfing Delhi but also contribute to global warming. Though the practice has been banned, implementation remains lackluster, and the age-old tradition perseveres due to the lack of affordable and convenient alternatives for farmers. The review from the University of Surrey and Delhi's regional government officials underscores the need for a cohesive approach involving Delhi and its rural neighbors in battling this grave health and environmental threat. By engaging the farming communities and addressing the problem of crop burning, it is possible to have a significant impact on Delhi's air quality. Creating a transitioning path for farmers with viable alternatives to crop burning - like using crop residues as a raw material for producing bio-energy or organic fertilizers - could be a potential solution. This needs governmental backing and support to be economically feasible for farmers. The review advocates for the development of collaboration and mutual understanding between urban and rural communities - making it fundamental to the success of any such initiatives. It further underlines the importance of education and awareness campaigns to apprise rural populations about the damaging effects of crop burning and the benefits of sustainable methods. In conclusion, Delhi's battle with air pollution is not only about its urban transformation or tightening environmental regulations, but also about engaging rural areas, breaking down the urban-rural divide, and creating mutually beneficial partnerships. The fight against smog is a shared responsibility that transcends city limits and requires collective action. The findings of this study therefore present a persuasive argument for holistic environmental strategies that consider the wider regional impact and work towards the common goal of sustainable, breathable air for all.
