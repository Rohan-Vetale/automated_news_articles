Bitcoin price hits record $69K after SEC approvals fueled $7B in investments

The constant evolution of the digital world continually brings forth events that carry significant implications; one such event being the Bitcoin halving. This intriguing occurrence, which unfolds approximately every four years, is one that brings about a considerable change in the rewards for those invested in Bitcoin mining. The repercussion of this event, seen through the lens of potential investors and existing stakeholders, is often perceived as a catalyst that will inevitably drive Bitcoin prices higher. Bitcoin halving can be better understood in relation to the fundamental law of supply and demand. Essentially, it is a process that reduces the rewards for miners by half, hence the term 'halving.' This phenomenon, ingrained in Bitcoin's code by its creator Satoshi Nakamoto, as a way of controlling inflation, occurs for every 210,000 blocks mined or approximately every four years. The potential implications of Bitcoin halving are multifaceted and attract different interpretations from various standpoints. One perspective belongs to that of the miners. For them, they see their reward for mining new blocks essentially slashed in half, which could potentially lead to a significant reduction in their profits, especially if the price of Bitcoin doesn't rise to offset the loss. However, there is a contrasting perspective, one held by the majority of investors and market speculators. They harbor the belief that Bitcoin halving will invariably lead to an increase in the price of the cryptocurrency. Their confidence is often hinged on the basic economic principle of supply and demand: with fewer bitcoins being generated, the demand for the digital currency will continue to grow, thus driving up the price. This theory is not bereft of past instances to back up its logic. Historical precedence gives it a certain credence, as it is known that after previous Bitcoin halvings, there has been a substantial upsurge in Bitcoin prices. For instance, the second Bitcoin halving event that occurred in July 2016 was followed by a bullish market taking Bitcoin from around $650 to ultimately reach nearly $20,000 by the end of 2017. Notwithstanding the optimism of investors, the crypto space remains notoriously volatile, with factors such as regulatory news, tech developments or macroeconomic trends that can affect its market value.
